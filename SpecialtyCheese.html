Specialty Cheese

Types of Specialty Cheese

  • Fresh Cheese - Popular examples: Mascarpone, Boursin Spreadable Cheese, Fresh Mozzarella)
  • Soft Cheese - Popular examples: Harbison, Brie, Camembert, Chevre
  • Semi-Soft Cheese - Popular examples: Young Gouda, Havarti, Fontina, Swiss
  • Semi-Firm Cheese - Popular examples: Edam, Cheddar, Gruyere, Comte
  • Hard Cheese - Popular Examples: Parmiggiano Reggiano, Aged Gouda, Pecorino Romano, Manchego
  • Blue Cheese - Popular Examples: Stilton, Roquefort, Gorgonzola

The Production Process

  • The first step in the cheese making process begins at the farm. The milk quality of the cow is one of the most essential components to high quality cheese. This step is also proof that no two specialty cheeses are the same. The traits of milk even will differ depending on the time of year it is produced.
  • The next step is acidifcation. Cheesemakers use their own blends of starter cultures and add it to the milk which changes the lactose into lactic acid.
  • After this the milk is coagulated. The milk starts to turn into a semi solid.
  • Once the milk begins to solidify it starts to form curd and why. This is where many different styles of cheese can greatly differ. The cheesemaker begins to cut the curd and depending on how small the curd is cut and how the curd is cut is what the end result will end up being. For a cheese like cheddar the curds are cut into cubes and cooked. This process is called "cheddaring."
  • The cheese makers will then begin the 'salting' process where they salt the cheese to the desired amount for flavor and to preserve it.
  • The cheese is then shaped. Some cheeses are shaped in 20 pound circles, others are 40 pound blocks. While not necessarily practical, some cheesemakers have created cheese wheels over 10,000 pounds! These wheels are known as 'mammoths' and are rare to find these days. However a specialty cheese company in Wisconsin called Hennings still makes these on special request.
  • The final step for the cheese is ripening. The person who ages cheese is called the affineur. Many cheese can be aged for over 10 years. Once this step is done all that is left is to eat!
